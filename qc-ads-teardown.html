Quick Commerce Ads:
A Product Manager's Teardown

Six platforms, one question: how do they actually make money from advertising? I looked at the formats, pricing, and targeting mechanics — and where each one is thinner than it looks.

By Deven Shah
Context Independent product analysis
Updated April 2026
Context

Why QC ads are a different product to design for

The consumer opening Blinkit already knows what they need. The whole funnel — impression, decision, checkout — happens in one session, usually under 10 minutes. That changes what you build.

Ad budgets have followed — QC went from 3–5% of digital spend to 15–20% in two years. QC platforms report 30% click-to-conversion versus 10% on standard Amazon. SKU-level attribution makes the ROI visible in a way brand campaigns never managed.

PM observation

Ad visibility here is inventory-dependent. OOS at your pincode's dark store? Your ad doesn't show — regardless of bid. No traditional ad server is built for this. Most ignore it and let the advertiser figure out why ROAS dropped.

Performance mechanics

Why most QC campaigns underperform

The most common failure: running QC ads like Google or Meta — national targeting, standard creative, same KPIs. QC is hyperlocal in a way those platforms aren't. A campaign that works in Koramangala can lose money in Malad. You can't abstract away the geography.

What actually works
  • ✓Stock availability at dark store level — ads only show when in stock at that pincode
  • ✓City-first, zone-level campaign structure rather than national blanket bids
  • ✓Time-band bidding — morning for dairy and breakfast, evening for snacks and beverages
  • ✓Local creative — city-specific copy consistently outperforms national messaging
What quietly destroys your numbers
  • ✗Running ads on out-of-stock SKUs — most common and costly mistake on QC platforms
  • ✗National campaigns ignoring pincode-level stock and demand variation
  • ✗Scaling cities before achieving 90%+ availability in the home city
  • ✗Copy-pasting campaigns across platforms without adapting to format mechanics
PM observation

Every brand has the same story: campaign ran, product went OOS, ROAS dropped, nobody flagged it until the weekly report. Auto-pause on OOS sounds like a two-day sprint. It isn't — real-time dark-store signals are hard to get and harder to wire into bidding logic. But advertisers who've been burned will pay a premium for the platform that solves it.

Gap analysis

Six gaps, in order of how interesting I find them

Something most teardowns miss

In September 2025, Blinkit moved to a first-party inventory model — it now buys and owns the stock. By Q3 FY26, ~90% of NOV runs through Blinkit's own inventory. Brands now pay for visibility on inventory Blinkit controls, not their own listings. The auction mechanics, ROAS attribution, and buyer relationship all shift. I don't know how this plays out long-term, but it's the thing I'd be most focused on.

On Swiggy Instamart's strategy shift

Blinkit added 272 dark stores in one quarter. Swiggy added 40. Swiggy's CFO said they'd double revenue by making stores bigger, not adding more — 8,000–10,000 sq ft vs standard 4,000. Bigger stores mean more SKU depth, more placement inventory, more auction competition. That should be driving ad product decisions. I'm not sure it is.

01
Inventory-bid coupling — nobody has actually solved it

Brands with hundreds of SKUs across thousands of pincodes are still doing this manually — spreadsheets, AM calls, post-hoc ROAS. The infrastructure is hard, but advertisers with this problem will pay for the platform that solves it.

02
Nobody is monetising the cart page properly

Instamart has checkout placements. Everyone else has nothing. The cart screen has the highest purchase intent in the app — the user has already decided to buy. Five out of six platforms are leaving that moment empty.

03
Three dashboards, no unified view

A brand on Blinkit, Zepto, and Instamart exports three reports and still can't get a clean QC ROAS number. The platform that solves this doesn't have to be one of the three — it just needs the integrations and the trust.

04
Brand stores — only Blinkit bothered

Five platforms, one brand store product. Blinkit built it; nobody else did. For any brand with a real portfolio, showing a full range converts better than a single sponsored listing.

05
Rs. 5 to 10L a quarter is a big door to close

D2C brands and regional FMCG players — the ones QC's hyperlocal model was made for — are priced out by Rs. 5–10L entry minimums. Nobody has built a self-serve tier for them yet.

06
QC advertising is still pre-programmatic

Most campaigns are still managed by account teams, paced manually, with reports that come days late. Real-time bidding and clean measurement APIs don't exist at the category level yet. Amazon Now is the only entrant with DSP infrastructure — whether that's an advantage or a mismatch depends on how different QC inventory actually is from standard display.

Product thinking

How I think about building in this space

Where would I start?
Sponsored search — not because it's interesting, but because advertisers know how to evaluate it. Get them to positive ROAS fast, then introduce formats they haven't bought before.
What is the hardest product problem?
Getting real-time inventory signals into the ad serving layer. It sounds like a data problem — it's actually a trust problem. If advertisers can't rely on the platform to stop charging when their SKU is OOS, they pull spend and don't come back.
How do I validate before building?
Mock up the advertiser dashboard and put it in front of 3–5 real brand managers before engineering touches it. A working prototype with fake data tells you what people actually look at — not what they say they want.
What is the right success metric?
90-day advertiser retention. Revenue is a lagging indicator — by the time it drops, you've already lost the relationship. If advertisers aren't returning after their first campaign, the ROAS story or the reporting is broken.
How do I balance monetisation with experience?
Track ad-to-cart rate alongside revenue. If that drops while revenue goes up, you're borrowing against user experience. Better to catch it at 5% degradation than 30%.
What would I build in year two?
A self-serve tier. D2C and regional brands — perfect for QC's hyperlocal model — can't afford Rs. 5L minimums. Lower the floor, automate optimisation, and you reach a segment the managed-service model structurally can't touch.
